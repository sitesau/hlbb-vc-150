Understanding the Costs: Who Pays?

The most common question buyers ask is whether a broker will cost them money. For standard residential lending, the answer is usually that the service is free to you because the lender pays the broker a commission. This commission is factored into the loan product offered to you. Australian law requires brokers to act in your best interests under the Best Interests Duty, which means the recommendation cannot simply be the loan that pays the highest commission. Instead, the broker must recommend a product that is suitable for your needs and financial goals. If you are a first home buyer, this duty ensures you are not steered toward a loan that is not right for your situation, even if it pays the broker more.

While standard owner-occupier and refinance loans typically attract no fee to the borrower, there are exceptions. Complex commercial or specialist lending might involve a fee, though a good broker will disclose this up front. If you are switching lenders to chase a sharper rate, you may still face discharge and establishment fees from your previous lender, but these are not charged by the broker. You should budget for the loan costs, such as establishment fees, stamp duty on the transfer of the property, and lenders mortgage insurance if your deposit is less than 20 per cent. These are the costs of the loan itself, not the service of finding it.

Brisbane first home buyers should budget for the full set of upfront costs, then offset them against the grants they qualify for. This includes the deposit, transfer stamp duty, conveyancing, building and pest inspections, and loan fees. On the other side of the ledger, the Queensland First Home Owner Grant supports eligible buyers of new homes, and federal guarantee schemes can let some buyers purchase with a smaller deposit without paying LMI. Refinancing and Equity Release

Refinancing is not only for people chasing the lowest headline rate. It can consolidate higher-interest debt, release equity for renovations, or move you onto a loan with an offset account that quietly saves interest every month. The test is simple: weigh the savings against discharge and establishment fees. A refinance review through the Home Loan Brokers Brisbane team will tell you fast whether switching stacks up.

When you refinance, you replace your existing home loan with a new one. This is often done to access a lower interest rate, reduce monthly repayments, or access the equity built up in your home for renovations. An interest-only loan can be suitable for investors who want lower monthly repayments while the property is being rented out. However, you must remember that during the interest-only period, you are not reducing the principal debt, which can impact how much you can borrow in the future. An offset account sits alongside your home loan and reduces the amount of interest you pay, as the balance is subtracted from your loan balance for interest calculations.

Switching lenders is not free. You will typically pay discharge fees to your previous lender and establishment fees to your new lender. A broker will calculate these costs against the potential interest savings over time. If the savings are significant, refinancing is usually worthwhile. If the costs are high and the rate difference is small, it may not make financial sense.
